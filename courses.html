Physics

This is a course taught by Amir Kashani Poor in the M2 of the ICFP master. We started by rederiving what was done in the Relativistic Quantum Mechanics and Quantum Field Theory course taught by Adel Bilal in the M1. We built step by step the theory of QED and finally the last part of the course focues on renormalization theory.

This is a course taught by Giulio Biroli in the M2 of the ICFP master. We started by studying stochastic dynamics and stochastic processes leading to the MSRDJ path integral formulation as well as the Onsager-Machlup path integral and the Feynmann-Kac theory. We then studied time reversal symmetry and fluctuation-dissipation relations. With which we then studied our first application, polymer models and collective phenomena. We then looked at Mean Field Theory and in particular the Bethe approximation as well as a quick introduction to real space RG, which we used to study topological defects such as the creation of vortices in the XY-model.

This is a course taught by Daniele Steer in the M2 of the ICFP master. The first half of the course was used to build the theory of General Relativity and re-derive Einstein's equations. Once we arrived at Einstein's equations we started studying all the different possible spherically symmetric solutions, in particular all the different types of black holes. We then moved on to gravitational waves and showed how they emerged as solutions of the Einstein equations. Finally the course will finish on introducing some theories of modified gravity.

This is a course taught by Adam Nahum in the M2 of the ICFP master. The course was devoted to the Renormalization Group and its applications. We started by introducing real space RG as well as studying its drawbacks. We then looked at statistical field theory, the mean field approximation, the 4-epsilon and 2+epsilon expansions to study the O(n) models at low dimensions.

This is a course that was split in two, the first half was taught by Alberto Rosso the second was taught by Rémi Monasson. In the first half we studied modern algorithms which are ubiquitous in physics today. We covered many algorithms used in many-body problems and complex systems, with special emphasis on Monte Carlo methods, molecular dynamics, and optimization in complex landscapes. The second half was focused on Bayesian statistics. We looked at statistical inference and machine learning tools to harness the growing availability of experimental data to design accurate models of the underlying, complex, strongly non-homogeneous and interacting systems.

This is a course taught by Pr. Adel Bilal in the ENS Master ICFP program that I am currently following. We introduced relativistic quantum mechanics by taking a semi-historical approach. We started from the postulates of both theories to develop the Klein- Gordon Equation. After noticing the failures of the Klein-Gordon equation we used Lie groups/algebras symmetry arguments to derive the Dirac equation. We are currently introducing Quantum Field Theory.

This is a course taught by Pr. Nick Kaiser in the ENS Master ICFP program that I am currently following. We started by reviewing special relativity and developping the concepts of differential geometry in curved space-time and derived the Einstein field equations. We then developped the weak-field theory of gravity and applied it to gravitational waves and discussed other applications including black-holes and elementary stellar structure theory ; gravitational lensing ; the solar system (precession of Mercury’s orbit) ; and the derivation of the singularity theorems from Raychaudhuri’s equation. The second half of the course is deidcated to cosmology and is still ongoing.

This is a course taught by Pr. Nathan Goldman in the ENS Master ICFP program that I followed. Mathematics

This is a coruse taught by David Hernandez in the M2 of the ICFP master. We started by defining groups, algebras, lie groups and lie algebras as well as their representations. We then looked at Cartan subspaces and used them to build the classification of all Lie Algebras in terms of their Dynkin diagrams. In doing so we studied weight spaces, the weight lattice with a special focus on representations that arise in physics such as the spinor representation of so(2n+1, C).

This is a course taught by Pr. Raphael Cerf in the ENS Master ICFP program that I followed. We started from the formalism behind modern probability theory and a quick overview of the important measure theory theorems without always proving them. We then moved on to random walks, the law of large numbers, the central limit theorem and finished with percolation.

This is a Bachelor 3 course of the ENS math department taught by Pr. Nicolas Bergeron. We started from the basics of complex analyis: definitions of holomorphic functions, Cauchy's Theorem, Goursat's Lemma. We then saw the main theorems of complex analysis: Liouville's theorem, zeros of analytic functions, Shwartz's Lemma, integral of holomorphic functions, Weierstrass products and so on. We then studied meromorphic functions and residues. The second half of the course focused on more in-depth topics such as: indices, conformal representations, coverings, modular functions, harmonic functions and elliptic modular functions.

This is a Bachelor 2 course of the Ecole Polytechnique. This course builds upon the topology notions studied in Topology and multivariable calculus to allow for a more profound study of functions. Examining functions as limits of simpler ones (e.g. for approximation problems) is made possible in a rigorous manner thanks to topological ideas. This provides the possibility of using crucial tools in many scientific fields; the most striking one being Fourier series. The second part of the course dealt with a wide array of differential equations.
